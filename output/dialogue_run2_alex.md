**Lesson 5: Correlation**

Correlation measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between the movements of two or more assets. It is quantified by a coefficient ranging from -1.0 to +1.0. 

- A correlation of **+1.0** (perfect positive correlation) means the assets move in perfect synchrony.
- A correlation of **-1.0** (perfect negative correlation) means the assets move in perfectly opposite directions.
- A correlation of **0** implies there is no linear relationship between their movements.

Understanding correlation is vital because it is the engine that drives the benefits of diversification. When assets are not perfectly correlated, the volatility of the combined portfolio is lower than the weighted average volatility of the individual assets.

**Lesson 8: Sharpe Ratio**

The Sharpe Ratio is a fundamental metric used to evaluate the risk-adjusted return of an investment. It helps investors understand whether the excess returns of a portfolio are due to smart investment decisions or simply due to taking on excessive risk. 

The ratio is calculated by subtracting the risk-free rate (the return on an investment with zero risk, such as a U.S. Treasury bill) from the portfolio's expected return, and then dividing that result by the portfolio's standard deviation.

**Formula:**
$$\text{Sharpe Ratio} = \frac{R_p - R_f}{\sigma_p}$$

Where:
- $R_p$ = Expected portfolio return
- $R_f$ = Risk-free rate
- $\sigma_p$ = Standard deviation of the portfolio's excess return

A higher Sharpe Ratio indicates a better risk-adjusted return, meaning the investor is receiving more return for each unit of volatility endured.
